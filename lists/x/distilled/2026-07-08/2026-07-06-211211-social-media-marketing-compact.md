  Large-Scale Religious Conversions in Sabah under the Administrations of Tun Datu Mustapha Harun and Syed Kechik, (1970–1975)
  By Avtar Singh
  Before the twentieth century, Islam had already reached the coastal regions of Sabah by around the fifteenth century through trade, intermarriage, and the influence of the Sultanates of Brunei and Sulu. Over a period of approximately 600 years, Islam spread gradually and was embraced by individuals, families, communities, and, in some instances, entire villages.
  Prior to 1963, however, the majority of indigenous peoples living in Sabah's interior—including the Kadazan, Dusun, Murut, Rungus, and other ethnic groups—continued to practise traditional animistic beliefs centred on ancestor veneration, nature spirits, and the Creator God, Kinorohingan. From the late nineteenth century onwards, Christian missionaries also succeeded in spreading Christianity throughout many parts of the interior.
  As a result, before 1967, Sabah's religious landscape generally reflected Islam as the dominant religion along much of the coastline, while Christianity and traditional indigenous beliefs were more widespread in the interior. Nevertheless, it would be inaccurate to suggest that Islam was entirely absent from the interior before 1963, as Muslim communities and individuals had long been present in certain inland areas.
  This situation changed significantly during the administration of Tun Datu Mustapha (1967–1975). Through missionary activities coordinated by the United Sabah Islamic Association (USIA) and supported by the Malaysian Government, the process of Islamisation became more organised and involved large numbers of indigenous people in the interior within a relatively short period. This marked a new phase in the history of Islam in Sabah, differing substantially from the gradual process of religious diffusion that had taken place over the previous six centuries.
  When discussing the large-scale conversions to Islam during the administrations of Tun Datu Mustapha and Tun Syed Kechik in the early 1970s, the reference is to the conversion of entire communities, villages, and families within a relatively short period. Several historical studies and contemporary reports also note that some of these conversion efforts were accompanied by offers of financial assistance, federal government development aid, and other forms of incentives made available to participating communities.
  
  Based on my interviews with former USNO and BERJAYA leaders, people who willingly embraced islam in the early 1970s during these mass conversions had a choice and were not necessarily compelled to convert to Islam.
  Whether these policies and practices were right or wrong is a matter open to interpretation and is not the focus of my discussion. From my perspective, I examine these events from a historical and sociological standpoint, viewing them as a process of social change that significantly shaped Sabah's religious landscape during the early 1970s.

  creative burnout is almost always a mental model or framework problem

  1. awareness levels

  …which means there are huge pockets of buyers they’ve never spoken to, simply because they never spoke to them differently
  
  there are 5 awareness levels, so just that times the 1 persona you’re speaking to opens up a whole new world of creative

  the most amount of your time as a marketer should go into research…
  
  …and within that research you’ll find key moments in time for your icp that act as catalysts for action…
  
  …so speaking to these specific moments will make your prospects feel very understood, which leads to more consumption, which leads to more conversions

  so obviously yes, everyone knows to test different hooks…
  
  …but the real unlock is being able to categorize your hooks
  
  so instead of just knowing “this hook worked,” it’s understanding at the fundamental level WHY it worked

  do social proof hooks give you lower cpa while relatability hooks bring in more clicks?
  
  utilizing hook mechanics and then being able to decipher the downstream effects is a major unlock you want in your back pocket as a marketer

  the most obvious, yet severely untapped by tons of marketers
  
  each icp consumes content differently…
  
  …which means there are formats certain audiences will resonate with, and others won’t
